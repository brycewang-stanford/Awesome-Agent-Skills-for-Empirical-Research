# Asset Allocation

Reference for understanding endowment portfolio composition, investment categories, and allocation strategies in NACUBO data.

> **Portal Scope Note:** Asset allocation data is **NOT available** in the Education Data Portal mirror. The Portal mirror contains only market value columns (`endow_total`, `endow_per_fte`, `endow_chg_mktval`). The asset allocation information in this file comes from the full NCSE study and provides contextual understanding for interpreting endowment market value changes observed in the Portal data.

## Overview

Endowment asset allocation describes how investment portfolios are divided across different asset classes. This is a key driver of investment returns and risk.

### Evolution of Endowment Investing

| Era | Dominant Strategy | Alternatives Allocation |
|-----|-------------------|------------------------|
| Pre-1980s | Stocks and bonds | <5% |
| 1980s-1990s | Diversification begins | 5-20% |
| 2000s | "Endowment Model" spreads | 20-40% |
| 2010s | Alternatives mainstream | 40-55% |
| 2020s | Peak alternatives | 50-60% |

### The Endowment Model

Pioneered by David Swensen at Yale, the "endowment model" emphasizes:
- Heavy allocation to alternative investments
- Diversification across uncorrelated assets
- Long time horizon exploitation
- Active manager selection
- Illiquidity premium capture

### Alternative Investments

Alternatives are non-traditional assets that typically offer:
- Lower correlation to public markets
- Potential for higher returns
- Illiquidity (premium compensation)
- Higher fees
- Access barriers

### Size-Allocation Pattern

**Key Insight**: Larger endowments have higher allocations to alternatives because:

1. **Access**: Minimum investments often $5M-$25M+
2. **Diversification**: Need size to diversify across managers
3. **Staff**: Require expertise to evaluate alternatives
4. **Liquidity**: Can tolerate longer lock-ups
5. **Track Record**: Relationships with top managers

**Private institutions** tend to have:

**Public institutions** may have:
- State investment restrictions
- More conservative mandates
- Fiduciary oversight constraints

## Dollar-Weighted vs Equal-Weighted

### Dollar-Weighted Allocation

Weights each institution by its endowment size:
- Dominated by large endowments
- Shows where most dollars are invested
- Higher alternative allocations

### Equal-Weighted Allocation

Treats each institution equally:
- Represents typical institution
- Higher traditional allocations
- More relevant for smaller endowments

**Example**: Dollar-weighted PE allocation might be 18%, while equal-weighted might be 10%.

- Manager selection (wide dispersion)

## Allocation Strategy Considerations

### Liquidity Management

Endowments must balance:
- Illiquid alternatives (PE, VC) with higher expected returns
- Liquid assets for spending distributions and capital calls
- Unexpected needs (financial crisis, opportunity)

**Rule of thumb**: Maintain 10-20% in liquid assets.

### Rebalancing

How institutions maintain target allocations:
- Calendar-based (quarterly, annually)
- Threshold-based (rebalance when drift exceeds X%)
- Opportunistic (market conditions)

### Target vs Actual

Most institutions set **target allocations** in their Investment Policy Statement (IPS) and allow ranges around targets:

| Asset Class | Target | Range |
|-------------|--------|-------|
| Private Equity | 15% | 12-18% |
| Venture Capital | 10% | 7-13% |
| Hedge Funds | 15% | 12-18% |

## Data Considerations

### Allocation Reporting

- Reported as of fiscal year end (June 30)
- Based on fair market value
- May include unfunded commitments differently
- Category definitions have evolved

### Comparability Issues

- Different institutions may classify same asset differently
- "Marketable alternatives" definition varies
- Global equity may overlap with US/international
- Real assets definition varies (REIT treatment)

### Alternative Valuation

Private assets valued using:
- GP (General Partner) marks
- Third-party valuations
- NAV reports (often lagged)

This can introduce smoothing and lag into returns.
