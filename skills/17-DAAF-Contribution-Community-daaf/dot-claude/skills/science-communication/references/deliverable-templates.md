# Deliverable Templates

Structural outlines, rules, and example skeletons for four core communication deliverable types. Each template is designed for a specific audience and purpose — choose based on who will read it and what decision it supports.

For audience-specific strategy, see `audience-analysis.md`. For narrative structure choices, see `narrative-frameworks.md`.

## 1. Executive Summary

**Audience:** Executives, board members, senior decision makers
**Purpose:** Deliver the bottom line and its implications in the shortest possible format
**Framework:** Pyramid Principle (answer first, then supporting logic)

### Structural Outline

| Section | Max Length | Content |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Bottom line** | 1-2 sentences | The single most important finding or recommendation. This is the answer. |
| **Key findings** | 3-5 bullets | Supporting evidence, one bullet per finding. Each bullet should stand alone. |
| **Methodology note** | 1-2 sentences | What data, what time period, what approach — in plain language. No jargon. |
| **Implications** | 2-3 bullets | What this means for the organization. Translate findings into strategic impact. |
| **Caveats** | 1-2 sentences | Only limitations that could change the recommendation. Frame as "what could change this picture." |
| **Recommended next steps** | 2-3 bullets | Specific, actionable items with owners if possible. |

### Rules

1. **Maximum 10% of full document length.** A 20-page report gets a 2-page executive summary. A 5-page brief gets a half-page summary.
2. **Self-contained.** The reader should never need to consult the full report to understand the executive summary. Every claim must be interpretable without additional context.
3. **No new information.** Everything in the executive summary must appear in the full document. The summary distills — it does not introduce.
4. **One page is ideal.** If it goes to two pages, it's not executive enough. Cut ruthlessly.
5. **Declarative titles on any embedded charts.** "Rural Districts Lost Teachers Fastest" not "Teacher Attrition by District Type."
6. **No methodology in the body.** The methodology note is a courtesy sentence, not a methods section. "We analyzed enrollment data for 13,000 districts over 8 years" is sufficient.

### Example Skeleton

> **Bottom line:** Early literacy interventions should replace class-size reduction as the district's primary reading improvement strategy, based on evidence showing 2-3x larger effects at 40% lower cost per student.
>
> **Key findings:**
> - Schools piloting structured literacy programs improved reading proficiency by 14 percentage points; schools with class-size reduction improved by 5 points
> - Cost per student served is $1,200 for literacy programs vs. $2,000 for class-size reduction
> - Effects are largest for students who entered below grade level — the highest-need population benefits most
>
> **Based on:** Analysis of 200 district reading programs (2019-2025), controlling for student demographics, baseline performance, and prior funding levels.
>
> **Implications:**
> - Current $14M class-size investment is producing below-benchmark returns
> - Redirecting $8M to literacy programs could serve 2x more students at higher effectiveness
> - Implementation timeline is 6-12 months vs. 2-3 years for further class-size changes
>
> **Caveat:** These findings are based primarily on elementary schools; middle school effects are less certain.
>
> **Next steps:**
> - Approve $2M pilot in 6 schools for 2026-27 (Board action needed by April)
> - Commission independent evaluation design by June
> - Report interim results to the Board in January 2027

## 2. Policy Brief

**Audience:** Policymakers, legislative staff, agency officials
**Purpose:** Inform a policy decision with evidence without prescribing a specific policy choice
**Framework:** SCQA (establish the problem, then present the evidence)

### Structural Outline

| Section | Max Length | Content |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Title** | 1 line | Descriptive but engaging. States the topic and finding direction, not the full conclusion. |
| **Summary box** | 3-5 bullets | Key takeaways in plain language. This is what gets read if nothing else does. |
| **Context** | 1-2 paragraphs | Why this issue matters now. What policy question is at stake. What the current landscape looks like. (SCQA: Situation + Complication) |
| **What the evidence shows** | 2-4 paragraphs + 1-2 figures | Core findings, each linked to a policy-relevant implication. Lead each paragraph with a numbers-free headline sentence (IES Strategy 2). |
| **Policy alternatives** | 2-4 paragraphs | Present options, not a single recommendation. For each alternative: what it does, what evidence supports it, what tradeoffs it involves. |
| **What we don't know** | 1 paragraph | Key limitations framed as open questions, not methodological confessions. "The evidence does not yet tell us whether..." |
| **Sources and methodology** | Brief appendix | Data sources, time period, approach. Available for staff who want to assess credibility. |

### Rules

1. **2-4 pages maximum.** Policy briefs that exceed 4 pages lose their audience. If you need more space, write a report and attach a 1-page summary.
2. **No jargon in the body.** Every technical term must be translated. See `plain-language.md`.
3. **Present alternatives, not prescriptions.** The researcher's role is to present evidence and illuminate tradeoffs. The policymaker's role is to decide. "Based on our analysis, three approaches merit consideration..." not "We recommend..."
4. **Lead each paragraph with the finding in plain language.** Follow IES Strategy 2: the first sentence of each evidence paragraph states the insight without numbers. Numbers come in the supporting sentences.
5. **Include only the most essential caveats** — those "too important to exclude" (IES Strategy 4). Over-caveating undermines confidence without serving the reader.
6. **Every figure must be self-interpreting.** Declarative title, clear labels, source note. A policymaker should understand the chart without reading surrounding text.
7. **Summarize, don't extrapolate.** Per IES Strategy 6: present what the evidence shows and its implications, not explicit policy recommendations.

### Example Skeleton

> **Title:** Early Literacy Programs Outperform Class-Size Reduction for Reading Improvement
>
> **Key Takeaways:**
> - Structured literacy programs improved reading scores 2-3 times more than class-size reduction
> - Literacy programs cost 40% less per student served
> - Benefits were largest for students entering below grade level
> - Evidence is based on 200 districts over 6 years; results were consistent across diverse contexts
>
> **Context:** Since 2005, states have invested heavily in reducing class sizes as a strategy for improving student outcomes. [Situation.] However, recent evidence suggests that *how* instructional time is used may matter more than *how many* students share the classroom. [Complication.]
>
> **What the evidence shows:**
> Schools using structured literacy programs saw substantially larger reading gains than those relying on class-size reduction alone. [Headline — no numbers.] Specifically, literacy program schools improved proficiency rates by 14 percentage points compared to 5 points for class-size reduction schools, after accounting for student demographics and baseline performance. [Supporting evidence.]
>
> The benefits were not uniform across all students. [Headline.] Students who entered below grade level showed the largest gains — 18 percentage points in literacy programs versus 4 points with smaller classes. [Supporting evidence.]
>
> **Policy alternatives:**
> - *Option A: Full reallocation* — redirect class-size funds to literacy programs...
> - *Option B: Phased transition* — pilot literacy programs alongside existing class-size commitments...
> - *Option C: Targeted deployment* — maintain class-size investments in early grades, redirect in upper elementary...
>
> **What we don't know:** The evidence does not yet tell us whether these effects persist beyond elementary school, nor whether the same programs work equally well in rural and urban settings.

## 3. Stakeholder Presentation

**Audience:** Mixed groups (board members, managers, practitioners, community members)
**Purpose:** Build shared understanding and move toward a decision or next steps
**Framework:** Three-Act Data Story or AIDA, depending on whether the goal is understanding or buy-in

### Structural Outline

| Section | Slides | Content |
|---------|--------|---------|
| **Opening hook** | 1 | A striking finding, a compelling question, or a human story that makes the topic immediate. Earn attention in the first 30 seconds. |
| **Context** | 1-2 | What is the situation? Why does this matter now? What question are we answering? |
| **Key findings** | 3-5 | One finding per slide. Declarative title at the top. One supporting chart or visual. 2-3 bullet points maximum. |
| **What it means** | 1-2 | Translate findings into implications for this specific audience. "So what?" for each finding. |
| **Recommendations / options** | 1-2 | What should happen next? Be specific. If the audience needs to decide, frame the decision clearly. |
| **Discussion** | 1 | Open with a specific question, not "any questions?" Try: "Given these findings, what concerns do you have about Option B?" |

### Rules

1. **One message per slide.** If you need two charts, use two slides.
2. **Slide titles are declarative sentences** that state the finding. "Rural Districts Lost Teachers Fastest" — not "Teacher Attrition Data." The title should tell the story even if the audience doesn't look at the chart.
3. **Maximum 3-5 bullets per slide, maximum 6-8 words per bullet.** Slides are visual aids, not documents. If you're reading your slides, you've written a document.
4. **Presenter notes, not slide text.** The detail goes in your spoken remarks, not on the slide. The audience should be listening to you, not reading ahead of you.
5. **Design for the back row.** Minimum 24pt for body text, 32pt for titles. If they can't read it from the back of the room, cut it.
6. **Consistent visual language.** Same color means the same thing across all slides. Same chart style throughout. See data-scientist visualization references for chart construction standards.
7. **Build in pauses for questions.** After key findings slides, pause. "Does this match your experience?" invites engagement and surfaces misunderstanding early.
8. **End with a specific ask or clear next step.** Never end with "Questions?" without first stating what you need from the audience.

### Example Skeleton

> **Slide 1 (Hook):** "Our highest-need students are falling further behind — but three schools found a way to reverse the trend." [Photo or simple chart showing the gap]
>
> **Slide 2 (Context):** The district invested $14M in class-size reduction since 2019. Reading scores have remained flat. [Simple trend line]
>
> **Slide 3 (Finding 1):** "Literacy programs produced 3x the reading gains of class-size reduction." [Paired bar chart, two bars]
>
> **Slide 4 (Finding 2):** "The biggest gains went to students who needed them most." [Chart showing effect by baseline level]
>
> **Slide 5 (Finding 3):** "Literacy programs cost 40% less per student." [Cost comparison chart]
>
> **Slide 6 (What it means):** Redirecting resources could serve twice as many students at higher effectiveness.
>
> **Slide 7 (Recommendation):** Proposed pilot: 6 schools, $2M, starting fall 2026. Board approval needed by April.
>
> **Slide 8 (Discussion):** "What would you need to see from the pilot to support district-wide expansion?"

## 4. Media Talking Points

**Audience:** Journalists and through them, the general public
**Purpose:** Equip journalists to tell an accurate story about your findings
**Framework:** SCQA (compressed) — the hook and the answer

### Structural Outline

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Headline finding** | One sentence that answers: "What is new?" Make it quotable. |
| **Why it matters** | One sentence that answers: "Why should readers care?" Connect to something the audience experiences. |
| **Supporting context** | 2-3 sentences with the most compelling supporting data. Concrete numbers, specific comparisons. |
| **Prepared analogies** | 1-2 analogies or comparisons that make abstract findings tangible. "That's the equivalent of..." |
| **Proactive caveat** | 1 sentence stating the most important limitation, framed constructively. Better to frame your own limitation than have someone else frame it. |
| **Quotable hedged statement** | A pre-prepared sentence you would be comfortable seeing in print, with appropriate uncertainty. |
| **Background (if asked)** | 2-3 sentences on methodology, available for journalists who want to assess credibility. |

### Rules

1. **Prepare for 1-2 quoted sentences.** Even from a 30-minute interview, most stories use 1-2 direct quotes. Make those quotes count. Practice saying them out loud until they're natural.
2. **No jargon.** Not even "statistically significant." Journalists will either skip the term or misdefine it.
3. **Provide the headline.** If you don't give the journalist a clear headline, they'll write their own — and it may not be the most accurate version of your finding.
4. **Prepare three tiers of depth.** (1) The headline in one sentence. (2) Supporting context in 2-3 sentences. (3) Full methodology on request. Let the journalist choose their depth.
5. **Proactively state limitations.** If you don't provide the caveat, it likely won't appear. But frame it constructively: "This study focused on elementary schools — we're now extending to middle schools" rather than "A limitation of this study is..."
6. **Analogies travel further than numbers.** "That's the equivalent of losing one full year of learning" is more quotable than "0.8 standard deviations below the mean."
7. **Never say "no comment."** If you can't answer, explain why and offer to follow up.

### Example Skeleton

> **Headline finding:** "Schools that switched from smaller classes to structured reading programs saw three times the improvement in student reading scores."
>
> **Why it matters:** "Millions of dollars are being spent on a strategy that produces smaller results than a cheaper alternative — and the students who need the most help are benefiting the least from the current approach."
>
> **Supporting context:** "We studied 200 school districts over six years. Districts that adopted early literacy programs saw reading proficiency rise by 14 percentage points. Districts that focused on reducing class sizes saw only a 5-point improvement. The difference was even larger for students who started behind."
>
> **Prepared analogy:** "The gap between the two approaches is like the difference between walking and driving — both get you there, but one is dramatically faster, and the students with the longest journey benefit the most from the faster route."
>
> **Proactive caveat:** "This evidence is strongest for elementary schools. We're now investigating whether the same pattern holds in middle schools."
>
> **Quotable hedged statement:** "Our analysis provides strong evidence that how instructional time is used matters more than class size for reading improvement — and that the students most in need benefit the most from structured literacy approaches."

## How to Distill: Turning Long Reports into Short Deliverables

One of the most common tasks: "I have a 15-page report and need a 1-page executive summary." The deliverable templates above describe the *output* format. This section describes the *process* of distillation.

### The Distillation Process

1. **Read the report's conclusion first.** The bottom line is usually buried at the end. Extract it — this becomes the opening sentence of your short deliverable.

2. **Identify the 3 most important findings.** Not the 3 most interesting, not the 3 you worked hardest on — the 3 that most directly answer the original research question or inform the audience's decision. If you can't choose 3, ask: "If the reader remembers only one finding, which one?"

3. **For each finding, extract: the claim, the evidence, and the implication.** Strip away methodological detail, robustness checks, and supporting analysis. These belong in the full report, not the summary.

4. **Translate vocabulary.** Run every sentence through the audience-appropriate vocabulary filter (see `plain-language.md`). Replace jargon. Shorten sentences. Use active voice.

5. **Cut methodology to one sentence.** "We analyzed enrollment data from 13,000 districts over 8 years" is usually sufficient. If more is needed, it goes in a brief appendix or "methodology note" box.

6. **Apply the caveat filter.** Include only caveats that could change the reader's interpretation of the bottom line. "More research is needed" is never a caveat worth including. "These results only apply to elementary schools" might be.

7. **Check: does the short version faithfully represent the long version?** The distilled deliverable must not say something the full report does not support. Simplification is acceptable; misrepresentation is not.

### What to Cut vs. What to Keep

| Cut | Keep |
|-----|------|
| Literature review and background | Bottom-line finding |
| Methodological detail | One-sentence methodology note |
| Robustness checks and sensitivity analyses | Key findings (3 max) |
| All but the most important caveats | Decision-relevant limitations |
| Data description and variable definitions | Implications and next steps |
| Tables with many rows/columns | One or two high-impact charts |
| "As expected" findings | Surprising or counterintuitive findings |

### Managing Nuance Loss

Distillation inevitably loses nuance. Manage this honestly:

- **Acknowledge what is lost.** "This summary covers the headline findings. For methodology, robustness checks, and subgroup analyses, see the full report."
- **Don't overstate in the short version.** If the full report says "the evidence suggests," the summary should not say "the evidence proves."
- **Provide a path to depth.** Always link or reference the full report for readers who want more.

## Cross-Deliverable Consistency

When a single analysis produces multiple deliverable types (e.g., a full report, an executive summary, a policy brief, and a presentation), maintain consistency:

- [ ] The same finding is stated the same way across all deliverables (adapted for audience, not contradicted)
- [ ] Numbers are identical everywhere they appear
- [ ] Charts use the same color coding and style across formats
- [ ] Caveats included in shorter deliverables are a subset of those in longer ones (never introduce new caveats in the summary)
- [ ] The "bottom line" is consistent: if the report says A, the executive summary cannot say B
