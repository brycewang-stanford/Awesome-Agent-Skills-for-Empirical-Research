## What is EDFacts?

EDFacts is the U.S. Department of Education's centralized data collection system that:

- Collects pre-K through grade 12 education data from all 50 states, DC, and territories
- Centralizes data from State Education Agencies (SEAs)
- Supports federal accountability, planning, and policymaking
- Reduces state reporting burden through consolidated submissions

### EDFacts Collection Structure

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| EDPass | Submission portal for state data uploads |
| EMAPS | Metadata and Process System |
| File Specifications | Technical requirements for data files |
| Data Groups | Standardized variable definitions |

### Three Collecting Offices

EDFacts data is collected by three different offices within the U.S. Department of Education:

| Office | Data Collected | Authorizing Law |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) | Common Core of Data (CCD) non-fiscal | Education Sciences Reform Act (ESRA) |
| Office of Elementary and Secondary Education (OESE) | ESEA/ESSA formula grant data | Elementary and Secondary Education Act |
| Office of Special Education Programs (OSEP) | IDEA special education data | Individuals with Disabilities Education Act |

## Legislative History

### Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA)

The foundational law governing federal K-12 education programs:

| Year | Version | Key Features |
|------|---------|--------------|
| 1965 | Original ESEA | Title I for disadvantaged students |
| 1994 | IASA | Standards-based reform |
| 2001 | No Child Left Behind (NCLB) | Accountability mandates |
| 2015 | Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) | State flexibility |

## NCLB Era (2002-2015)

The No Child Left Behind Act introduced high-stakes accountability:

### Adequate Yearly Progress (AYP)

| Requirement | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Annual testing | Grades 3-8 and once in high school |
| 100% proficiency goal | All students proficient by 2013-14 |
| Annual targets | Progressive increases toward 100% |
| Subgroup accountability | Each subgroup must meet targets |
| Consequences | Escalating sanctions for schools not meeting AYP |

### AYP Consequences (Escalating)

1. **Year 1**: School identified for improvement
2. **Year 2**: School choice required
3. **Year 3**: Supplemental Educational Services (tutoring)
4. **Year 4**: Corrective action (staff changes, curriculum)
5. **Year 5**: Restructuring (conversion, state takeover)

### Highly Qualified Teacher (HQT)

NCLB required all teachers to be "highly qualified":
- Bachelor's degree
- Full state certification
- Demonstrated competence in subject area

### Problems with NCLB

| Issue | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| Unrealistic 100% goal | All schools eventually "failing" |
| Teaching to the test | Narrowed curriculum |
| Gaming the system | Strategic test exclusions |
| Punitive approach | Focus on sanctions, not support |
| One-size-fits-all | Ignored state differences |

### NCLB Waivers (2011-2015)

As the 2013-14 deadline approached, states received flexibility waivers:
- 43 states received waivers
- States could design own accountability systems
- Maintained testing requirements
- Bridged gap until ESSA passage

## ESSA Era (2015-Present)

The Every Student Succeeds Act replaced NCLB in December 2015:

### Key ESSA Changes

| NCLB | ESSA |
|------|------|
| Federal prescription | State flexibility |
| AYP with fixed targets | State-designed goals |
| Single proficiency metric | Multiple indicators required |
| Federal sanctions | State-designed interventions |
| HQT requirements | State teacher quality decisions |

### ESSA Accountability Requirements

States must include these indicators:

| Indicator | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Academic achievement | Proficiency on state assessments |
| Academic progress | Growth or other measure (elementary/middle) |
| Graduation rate | 4-year ACGR (high school) |
| English learner progress | EL proficiency progress |
| School Quality/Student Success | At least one additional indicator |

### School Quality/Student Success Indicators

States can choose from options like:
- Chronic absenteeism
- School climate surveys
- Access to advanced coursework
- Postsecondary readiness
- College enrollment rates

### ESSA School Identification

| Category | Definition | Intervention |
|----------|------------|--------------|
| Comprehensive Support (CSI) | Bottom 5% of Title I schools | Needs improvement plan |
| Targeted Support (TSI) | Consistently underperforming subgroups | Targeted intervention |
| Additional Targeted Support (ATSI) | TSI schools performing as low as CSI | Additional resources |

### Long-Term vs. Interim Goals

ESSA requires states to set:

| Goal Type | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Long-term goals | Ambitious goals for proficiency and graduation |
| Interim measures | Progress checkpoints toward long-term goals |
| Subgroup goals | Specific goals to close achievement gaps |

## Key Accountability Concepts

### N-Size (Minimum Group Size)

The minimum number of students required to calculate and report an indicator:

| Consideration | Impact |
|---------------|--------|
| Too low n-size | Privacy concerns, unreliable statistics |
| Too high n-size | Excludes small schools, masks subgroup performance |
| State variation | States set n-size from 10 to 30+ |
| Trade-off | Statistical reliability vs. subgroup visibility |

### Participation Rate

ESSA requires 95% assessment participation:

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Minimum rate | 95% of students must be assessed |
| Consequences | States must address low participation |
| Subgroup participation | Applies to each subgroup |
| Opt-out impact | High opt-out rates reduce participation |

### Safe Harbor (NCLB)

Under NCLB, schools could meet AYP through "safe harbor":
- Show 10% reduction in students below proficient
- Applied when school missed proficiency target
- Eliminated under ESSA

### Confidence Intervals

Some states use confidence intervals in accountability:
- Account for statistical uncertainty
- Especially important for small schools
- Can affect school identification

## Federal vs. State Authority

### Federal Role

| Area | Federal Authority |
|------|-------------------|
| Testing requirement | States must test in reading/math grades 3-8 and high school |
| Subgroup reporting | Must report by race, disability, EL, economic status |
| Participation rate | 95% participation required |
| Indicator categories | Must include specified indicator types |
| Lowest performers | Must identify bottom 5% and underperforming subgroups |

### State Authority Under ESSA

| Area | State Decision |
|------|----------------|
| Assessment content | States design own tests |
| Proficiency cut scores | States set proficiency thresholds |
| Long-term goals | States set ambitious but achievable goals |
| Indicator weights | States determine how indicators combine |
| N-size | States set minimum group size |
| Interventions | States design support for identified schools |
| Additional indicators | States choose School Quality measures |

## Data Collection Offices

### NCES (National Center for Education Statistics)

Collects non-fiscal education data via CCD:
- School and district directory information
- Enrollment counts
- Staff counts
- Membership data

### OESE (Office of Elementary and Secondary Education)

Collects ESEA/ESSA program data:
- Assessment results
- Graduation rates
- Title I participation
- Accountability indicators

### OSEP (Office of Special Education Programs)

Collects IDEA special education data:
- Children with disabilities counts
- Educational environments
- Discipline data
- Outcomes data

## Implications for Data Analysis

### Pre-ESSA vs. Post-ESSA

- Different accountability frameworks
- Different state reporting requirements
- Avoid direct comparison across the transition

### State Plan Variations

Each state's ESSA plan differs:
- Check state-specific definitions
- Understand state's indicator weights
- Know state's n-size threshold

### Interpreting Accountability Status

School identification status reflects:
- State's specific methodology
- State's chosen thresholds
- Not comparable across states
