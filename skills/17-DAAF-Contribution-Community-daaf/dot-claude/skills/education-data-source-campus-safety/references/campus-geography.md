# Campus Geography: Location Categories for Clery Reporting

## Overview

The Clery Act requires institutions to report crime statistics for specific geographic categories. Understanding these definitions is critical for proper classification and for interpreting published statistics.

## The Four Geographic Categories

### 1. On-Campus

#### Definition

Any building or property owned or controlled by an institution within the same reasonably contiguous geographic area and used by the institution in direct support of, or in a manner related to, the institution's educational purposes, including residence halls.

**Plus**: Any building or property that is within or reasonably contiguous to the area described above, that is owned by the institution but controlled by another person, is frequently used by students, and supports institutional purposes.

#### Key Terms

**Owned or Controlled**:
- Owned: Institution holds title to property
- Controlled: Institution has significant control through lease, rental agreement, written agreement (including email), or other arrangement

**Reasonably Contiguous**:
- Properties are in relatively close proximity
- Form part of the institution's "campus" area
- No absolute distance standard; determined by institutional circumstances
- May include non-adjacent properties if within same general campus area

**Educational Purposes**:
- Instructional buildings
- Administrative buildings
- Student services facilities
- Research facilities
- Libraries
- Athletic facilities
- Student unions

#### Examples of On-Campus Property

**Always On-Campus**:
- Main campus buildings and grounds
- Residence halls and dormitories
- Academic buildings
- Libraries
- Student centers
- Campus recreation facilities
- Parking lots on campus
- Walkways and common areas on campus

**May Be On-Campus**:
- Leased buildings used for institutional purposes
- Shared facilities (institution has control or ownership)
- Athletic facilities (if owned/controlled and contiguous)
- Research facilities (if owned/controlled and contiguous)

### 2. On-Campus Student Housing Facilities

#### Definition

Any student housing facility that is owned or controlled by the institution, or is located on property that is owned or controlled by the institution, and is within the reasonably contiguous geographic area that makes up the campus.

#### Key Points

**Subset of On-Campus**:
- All on-campus student housing is also counted in "on-campus" totals
- Statistics are reported separately to highlight residential crime
- On-campus student housing is reported as a **subset**, not a separate category

**Owned or Controlled**:
- Institution owns the facility
- Institution leases or has written agreement for the facility
- Institution has significant control over the facility

**Within Campus Geographic Area**:
- Must be within the reasonably contiguous campus area
- Off-campus housing NOT owned/controlled by institution is NOT included
- Even if students live there, private apartments off-campus don't count

#### Examples

**On-Campus Student Housing**:
- Traditional residence halls
- Campus apartments owned by institution
- Fraternity/sorority houses owned by institution
- Graduate student housing on campus
- Family housing on campus

**NOT On-Campus Student Housing**:
- Off-campus apartments (even if popular with students)
- Privately owned fraternity/sorority houses off campus
- Housing cooperatives not controlled by institution
- Hotels used temporarily for student housing (unless controlled by institution)

### 3. Noncampus Building or Property

#### Definition

Any building or property owned or controlled by a student organization that is officially recognized by the institution; or

Any building or property owned or controlled by an institution that is used in direct support of, or in relation to, the institution's educational purposes, is frequently used by students, and is not within the same reasonably contiguous geographic area of the institution.

#### Two Categories

**1. Student Organization Properties**:
- Owned or controlled by officially recognized student organization
- Organization must be recognized by institution
- Examples: Off-campus fraternity/sorority houses, student organization retreat facilities

**2. Separate Campus Properties**:
- Owned or controlled by institution
- Used for educational purposes
- Frequently used by students
- NOT within the contiguous campus area

#### Examples of Noncampus Property

**Student Organization Properties**:
- Off-campus fraternity house (if owned/controlled by recognized org)
- Off-campus sorority house (if owned/controlled by recognized org)
- Student organization-owned retreat center

**Institutional Noncampus Property**:
- Satellite campuses (if not separately accredited)
- Research facilities away from main campus
- Farm or agricultural facilities
- Clinical sites with institutional agreement
- Off-campus classroom buildings
- University-operated study abroad facilities
- Off-campus athletic facilities

#### Study Abroad and Travel

**Study Abroad Programs**:
- Institution-sponsored programs at facilities controlled by institution: Noncampus
- Third-party programs with written agreement: May be noncampus depending on control
- Student-arranged independent study: Generally NOT Clery geography

**Repeated Use Standard**:
- Property must be "frequently used by students"
- One-time events generally don't create Clery geography
- Repeated, scheduled use does create Clery geography

### 4. Public Property

#### Definition

All public property, including thoroughfares, streets, sidewalks, and parking facilities, that is within the campus or immediately adjacent to and accessible from the campus.

#### Key Terms

**Public Property**:
- Property owned by a government entity
- Open to public access
- Streets, sidewalks, parks

**Within the Campus**:
- Public streets running through campus
- Public sidewalks within campus boundaries
- Public parks within campus area

**Immediately Adjacent**:
- Directly bordering campus property
- The sidewalk and street in front of campus buildings
- Public areas right at the campus edge

**Accessible From Campus**:
- Can be reached directly from campus
- No intervening private property
- Natural route students would take

#### Examples of Public Property

**Included**:
- Public sidewalk directly in front of campus building
- Public street running through campus
- Public park immediately adjacent to campus
- Public parking garage next to campus

**NOT Included**:
- Public streets not adjacent to campus
- Public parks across town
- Public property separated from campus by private property
- Public property that is not accessible from campus

#### Practical Determination

**General Rule**: One "block" from campus boundary
- Public property immediately bordering campus
- Streets directly in front of campus
- Does NOT extend indefinitely into the community

## Reporting by Geographic Category

### Statistics Structure

Crime statistics are reported separately for each geographic category:

| Category | Reported Separately | Includes Housing Subset? |
|----------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| On-Campus | Yes | Yes (housing shown as subset) |
| On-Campus Student Housing | Yes (as subset) | N/A |
| Noncampus | Yes | No |
| Public Property | Yes | No |

### Example Table Format

| Crime | On-Campus Total | On-Campus Housing | Noncampus | Public Property |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Burglary | 15 | 12 | 2 | 0 |

In this example:
- 15 total burglaries on campus
- 12 of those 15 were in student housing
- 2 burglaries at noncampus locations
- 0 burglaries on public property

### Multi-Campus Institutions

Institutions with multiple separate campuses report statistics separately for each campus if:
- The campus has its own identification number (OPEID)
- The campus produces separate statistics

**Branch Campuses**:
- Each branch with a separate OPEID has separate statistics
- Main campus statistics don't include branch campus crimes

## Classification Challenges

### Determining "Controlled"

**Factors Indicating Control**:
- Written agreement (lease, contract, MOU)
- Institution pays for the facility
- Institution provides security
- Institution enforces rules at the facility
- Institution maintains the facility

**Minimal Control ≠ Clery Control**:
- Merely registering a student organization doesn't make their property controlled
- Recognition must come with actual control over property

### "Reasonably Contiguous" Determination

**Factors to Consider**:
- Physical proximity
- Walking distance
- Natural geographic barriers
- Historical campus boundaries
- How the institution holds out the area

**No Bright-Line Rule**:
- Depends on specific institutional circumstances
- Large rural campuses may have different standards than urban campuses

### Frequently Used by Students

For noncampus property:
- Regular, scheduled classes or activities
- Regular use patterns
- Not just occasional events
- More than one-time or infrequent use

## Special Situations

### Leased Spaces

**Office Space in Commercial Building**:
- Institution leases office space: On-campus or noncampus (depending on location)
- Building common areas: May or may not be Clery geography (depends on control)

**Shared Research Facilities**:
- Institution has designated space: That space is Clery geography
- Common areas: Depends on level of institutional control

### Short-Term Arrangements

**Hotels for Overflow Housing**:
- If institution has written agreement and control: May be noncampus
- If students independently book: NOT Clery geography

**Conference Venues**:
- One-time event: Generally NOT Clery geography
- Regular, repeated use with control: May be noncampus

### Online/Distance Education

**Fully Online Students**:
- Students' homes are NOT Clery geography
- Institution doesn't control students' personal spaces
- Online-only programs still require campus facilities reporting

**Hybrid Programs**:
- Physical locations used for in-person components: Standard Clery geography rules apply

## Common Mistakes

### Over-Inclusion

- Counting all student activity off campus
- Including private property where students live
- Including public property far from campus

### Under-Inclusion

- Forgetting student organization properties
- Missing satellite facilities
- Not counting leased properties

### Misclassification

- Counting noncampus as on-campus
- Counting public property as on-campus
- Missing the student housing subset
